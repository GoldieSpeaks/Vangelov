Designing and specifying a multi-tenant agent portal for international cross-border payments — enabling counsellors at the world's largest education placement agency to generate secure payment links for students across dozens of countries and hundreds of universities.

The education payments ecosystem lacked a dedicated platform for agency-driven student payments. Counsellors needed to generate secure payment URLs covering tuition, accommodation, living expenses, and visa fees. 6 Overview
Executive Summary

This project defined the complete product requirements for a new Agent Payment Platform serving the world's largest international education placement agency. The platform enables counsellors (agents) to generate secure payment links for students, supporting tuition, accommodation, living expenses, and visa fees across all onboarded institutions.

The platform integrates directly with existing transaction processing infrastructure — reusing established database systems, operations pipelines, and file routing mechanisms rather than building standalone systems. Two payment flows were designed: agent-initiated (~90% of volume) where counsellors generate and send payment links, and student-initiated (~10%) where students discover the provider directly.

The architecture supports up to five payment product categories per transaction, role-based permissions across four organisational levels, and multiple payment methods including card processing, bank transfers, and regional alternative payment methods. The multi-tenant design deliberately supports future agency expansion without structural changes.

8 Scope
Project Scope

In Scope (Phase 1)

  • Agent portal with authentication, 2FA, and profile management
  • Payment link generation (up to 5 product categories per transaction)
  • Student payment form with T&C acceptance
  • Dual payment flows (agent-initiated + student-initiated)
  • Four-level role-based permissions hierarchy
  • Integration with existing transaction database and operations pipeline
  • Product-to-beneficiary bank account mapping
  • Email notifications (confirmation, reminder, receipt)

Out of Scope / Future Phases

  • Automated refund processing
  • Agency CRM-to-CRM API integration
  • Native mobile application
  • Agency self-registration (due diligence required)
  • Commission visibility in agent portal
  • Advanced dashboard and year-over-year reporting
  • University self-onboarding

12 Learnings
Lessons & Insights
● What Worked Well

Comprehensive PRD Before Development

Producing a detailed product requirements document covering the full platform vision before beginning development ensured all stakeholders were aligned on scope, hierarchy, and phased delivery before any code was written. This prevented costly mid-development scope changes.

● What Worked Well

Agency-Agnostic Design Principle

Building the multi-tenant hierarchy as a platform feature rather than an agency-specific implementation means additional agencies can be onboarded in the future without structural changes, maximising the return on development investment.

● Challenge

Open Questions Requiring Partner Input

Several critical decisions (exact data fields, link expiration timeframes, address book sharing, document collection) required input from the agency partner. Systematic tracking and resolution through recurring weekly meetings prevented them from blocking progress.

● Key Learning

Integration Over Greenfield

Choosing to build on existing transaction databases and operations pipelines rather than creating standalone systems significantly reduced scope, risk, and delivery time while maintaining consistency with established processing workflows.

● Risk Identified

Geo-Location Dependencies

The student-initiated flow (~10% of volume) depends on the agency's website behaviour in specific geographies. Geo-location restrictions affecting the inquiry button could block the direct payment path for certain regions, requiring partner technical investigation.

● Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Would have pushed for earlier resolution of the branch structure data from the partner. The mapping of regions, countries, and branches is foundational to the role-based permissions model, and delays in receiving this data create downstream dependencies in development planning.
