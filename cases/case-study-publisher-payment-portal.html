Payment Integration
Publisher Payment Portal
I designed and delivered a custom payment portal for a major academic publisher — enabling direct book and course purchases through a cross-border payment platform for the first time, with multi-provider integration spanning card payments, local payment methods, and bank transfers across multiple regions.

I identified the business opportunity when two major academic publishers requested the ability to sell books and courses directly to individuals and companies through our platform — which had no mechanism for product purchases, exclusively handling tuition and fee payments. I led the creation of an entirely new payment model — a dedicated login role, custom forms, product-to-beneficiary mapping, and regional payment provider integrations — navigating a mid-project architectural pivot across a 5-month lifecycle.

5 Overview
Executive Summary

I identified a business opportunity when two major academic publishers required the ability to sell books and courses directly through the cross-border payments platform. The platform had no existing capability for product-based transactions — it was built exclusively for student tuition payments with tightly coupled screening, beneficiary mapping, and notification logic.

I drove a significant mid-course pivot when I recognised that the initial batch-payment approach would not accommodate the publisher's product catalog model. I proposed and led adoption of a standalone payment portal with a dedicated login role, product-to-beneficiary mapping, multi-currency support, and integrations with four regional payment providers. I coordinated the BRD through 47 revisions to achieve stakeholder sign-off.

I delivered the solution within a 5-month lifecycle spanning initial ideation through production release. The reusable payment model I designed is now being extended to a second academic publisher, confirming the architecture's scalability.

7 Delivery
Project Timeline

End-to-end delivery from demand identification through release, navigating a major architectural pivot mid-project.

Week 1
Demand Identification & Initial Scoping
I identified the business opportunity from publisher client requests. I held the initial ideation call to explore batch-payment and agent-portal approaches. I flagged constraints around card payment limits and rate calculation logic early.
Weeks 2-3
Payments Team Analysis
I conducted deep-dive sessions with payments operations to understand current invoice and bulk processing flows. I mapped settlement patterns and identified that existing batching logic would not support product-level granularity required by publishers.
Weeks 3-4
Architecture Exploration
I evaluated multiple approaches: extending existing forms, deploying standalone pages, modifying agent portal logic. I gathered statistics on existing page usage patterns and made the decision to build a completely new form that would not conflict with existing payment flows.
Weeks 5-6
Payment Provider Validation
I conducted compliance validation with four regional payment providers. Two confirmed feasibility, one required flow documentation review, one I excluded due to unfavorable tax implications. I established the provider coverage map for target regions.
Week 8
Architectural Pivot
I executed the major project pivot, abandoning the initial batch-payment approach. I proposed and drove adoption of the new standalone portal model with dedicated login role, product-to-beneficiary mapping, and separate form architecture. I initiated mockups for the new approach.
Weeks 9-10
Technical Design Convergence
I facilitated weekly design calls to refine the product-beneficiary model. I agreed the approach of saving beneficiaries as hidden product attributes. I specified new database columns for distinguishing individual vs. company payments and defined login role exceptions for link-level visibility control.
Weeks 11-12
BRD Finalization & Notification Design
I drove the BRD through final revisions toward stakeholder sign-off. I specified the complete email notification redesign — removing student references across payment instructions, confirmations, and beneficiary notifications for both bank transfer and card payment channels.
Weeks 12-18
Development & Integration
I managed development execution across 21 sub-tasks: login role creation, payment form modifications, custom payment logic, beneficiary mapping, demo link, provider integrations, staff login modifications, payment history, and filtration capabilities.
Weeks 18-20
Testing & Client Preparation
I coordinated internal and external test reports. I drafted and reviewed client communication letters, confirmed default margin configurations with business stakeholders, and completed release preparation and version assignment.
Week 20+
Production Release & Extension
I released the portal to production and finalised the risk assessment, RAID log, and security documentation. I initiated the second publisher engagement using the same portal model, confirming architectural scalability. Ongoing provider expansion in progress.

10 Decisions
Key Decisions
When Decision Type
Week 4 Build standalone portal, not extend existing forms. New payment form deployed independently to avoid conflicts with existing tuition payment logic. Clean separation enabled faster development and testing. Strategic
Week 6 Exclude provider with unfavorable tax rates. One regional payment provider had tax implications that made it commercially unviable for this use case. Excluded from Phase 1; remaining three providers covered target regions adequately. Strategic
Week 8 Abandon batch-payment model, adopt product-beneficiary mapping. Mid-project pivot. Initial approach could not accommodate product catalog requirements. New model links products to beneficiaries via hidden fields, enabling multi-product, multi-beneficiary routing. Technical
Week 9 Single login role with column-level distinction for individual/company. Instead of creating separate roles, a single dedicated role differentiates payment types through database columns. Reduces role proliferation and simplifies permissions management. Technical
Week 10 Link-level visibility exceptions for login role. New role only appears on specific publisher links rather than globally. Prevents contamination of other payment flows while allowing targeted deployment. Technical
Week 11 Generic notification templates replacing student references. Rather than maintaining parallel templates, all notifications redesigned with generic terminology. Enables reuse across future payment types without template duplication. Process

12 Learnings
Lessons & Insights
● What Worked Well

Early Pivot Prevented Wasted Effort

Recognizing at week 8 that the batch-payment approach would not satisfy publisher requirements — and making a clean pivot to a standalone model — prevented months of development effort on a solution that would have needed replacement. The weekly call cadence made the pivot possible because alignment was already established across teams.

● What Worked Well

Reusable Architecture

The product-to-beneficiary mapping model proved architecturally sound. The second publisher engagement uses the identical pattern with only configuration-level changes. This validated the design decision to build a general-purpose model rather than a client-specific solution.

● Challenge

Payment Provider Complexity

Each regional payment provider had unique compliance requirements and restrictions on payment types they would support. Validating four providers in parallel required significant coordination with legal, compliance, and commercial teams. This workstream became the longest dependency in the critical path.

● Key Learning

47 BRD Revisions Indicate Scope Discovery

The extremely high revision count on the requirements document reflects how much scope was discovered during analysis rather than upfront. For projects entering genuinely novel territory, earlier prototyping or proof-of-concept work might reduce the BRD iteration cycle by surfacing constraints before specification.

● Risk Identified

Generic vs. Specific Notification Tradeoff

Moving to generic notification templates required changes across all payment channels. While the long-term benefit is clear (reusability), the short-term risk was introducing regressions in existing student notification flows. Careful scoping to Phase 1 and explicit exclusion of changes to existing templates mitigated this.

● Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Would have conducted the payment provider validation exercise in parallel with the initial architecture exploration rather than sequentially. Provider constraints ultimately influenced the architectural decision, and knowing them earlier would have shortened the path to the pivot by several weeks.
