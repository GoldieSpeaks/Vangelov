IODM Multi-Partner Dashboard Integration
I designed the product architecture and compliance framework that enabled a global payments analytics dashboard to support third-party partnership clients — unlocking multi-account visibility, partner-branded environments, and a reusable model that unblocked four downstream projects.

IODM is a third-party debt collection company that recovers outstanding student payments on behalf of US universities. When they partnered with TransferMate Education, their operating model introduced a product challenge: each university needed visibility across multiple accounts within a single dashboard — a capability the platform had never supported. I defined the product model, resolved cross-functional compliance concerns through 13+ stakeholder calls, designed a scalable partnership framework, and coordinated delivery across multiple teams to enable IODM's successful onboarding and establish the blueprint for all future partnership integrations.

5 Overview
Executive Summary

IODM is a debt collection company specialising in recovering outstanding student payments on behalf of US universities. When they chose to migrate from their existing payment provider to TransferMate Education, their operating model introduced a fundamental product challenge: the analytics dashboard was designed around a one-account-per-client model, but IODM's universities each needed visibility across multiple accounts within a single view.

This was not a simple UI feature. It required cross-functional product design spanning compliance, finance, operations, and two development teams. The challenge involved resolving regulatory concerns about multi-account representations, designing a partnership model that wouldn't break existing client experiences, accommodating legacy data quality issues, and ensuring the solution would serve as a reusable framework for future partnerships.

The user-facing deliverable was a filter. But the 90% of effort beneath it was the product model, the compliance framework, the stakeholder alignment, and the specification work that made the filter meaningful and trustworthy.

IODM successfully migrated from their previous provider and began processing payments through the platform. The partnership framework now enables onboarding additional third-party partners without requiring further product redesign — a reusable model for an entirely new category of client relationship.

8 Challenge
The Product Challenge

Why a "simple filter" required 13+ cross-functional calls, compliance resolution, and a new product model.

On the surface, the requirement was straightforward: let IODM's university clients see both their tuition payments and their debt recovery payments in one dashboard. In practice, this exposed five interconnected challenges:

1. The platform assumed one account = one client. The entire analytics experience was built around this assumption. Supporting multi-account visibility required rethinking the fundamental relationship model without breaking any existing client's experience.

2. Compliance concerns. Regulatory stakeholders raised valid concerns about how multi-account clients would be represented for reporting purposes. The proposed model needed to satisfy audit requirements while enabling the operational flexibility the partnership demanded.

3. Legacy constraints. Existing platform structures had dependencies from other clients that made modification risky. The solution had to work alongside existing structures, not replace them.

4. Data quality. Historical data had inconsistencies that made automated migration unreliable without manual validation and cleanup.

5. Downstream impact. Four other projects in the pipeline depended on how this problem was solved. Getting the model wrong would cascade blockers across multiple workstreams.

9 Decisions
Key Decisions
Phase Decision Type
Discovery Build alongside existing structures, not on top of them. Existing platform components had downstream dependencies from other clients. Rather than modifying what existed (and risking breakage), the solution was designed as an additive layer that coexists with the current model. Technical
Discovery Scalable model over point solution. The initial temptation was to hard-code a solution for IODM specifically. Instead, designed a reusable partnership framework that accommodates unlimited future partners without further product changes. Higher upfront effort, lower total cost. Strategic
Compliance One-to-one relationship between partners and institutional accounts. Satisfies regulatory audit requirements (each account maps to one identifiable entity) while enabling the multi-account visibility needed at the dashboard level. Resolved a weeks-long compliance impasse. Compliance
Scoping Phase 1 scoped to API partnership clients only. Extending the model to all partner types would have introduced significant additional complexity. Narrow scope to prove the model with the initial partners, then expand. De-risks delivery without limiting the framework. Strategic
Delivery Separate credentials per client institution. Each university gets its own access credentials rather than sharing through a single partner account. Provides proper access control, audit trail, and operational separation per institution. Strategic
Delivery Manual partner setup initially, admin interface deferred. Building an interface for partner management adds scope without immediate ROI. Manual process sufficient for the first partners. Interface prioritised for a later phase once the model is proven in production. Strategic
10 Complexity
Complexity & Constraints

The visible deliverable was a dashboard filter. The real complexity was beneath it: the cross-functional alignment, the compliance framework, and the product model that makes the filter trustworthy.

● Constraint

Cannot Modify Existing Structures

Existing platform components had live dependencies from other clients. Any modification risked breaking their established workflows. All partnership capability had to be achieved through additive design, not modification of existing components.

● Constraint

Regulatory Compliance Requirements

The proposed multi-account model raised regulatory reporting concerns. The resolution required designing a model where each account relationship could be audited independently — satisfying compliance while enabling the operational flexibility the business needed.

● Risk

Historical Data Inconsistencies

Legacy data had quality issues including duplicates and inconsistencies. Automated migration could not be trusted without validation. Mitigated through phased rollout with manual verification for the initial dataset.

● Dependency

Four Downstream Projects Blocked

Disbursement reconciliation, refund flows, commission reporting, and another integration project all depended on how this problem was solved. Getting the model wrong would cascade blockers across multiple concurrent workstreams.

● Constraint

Five-Function Stakeholder Alignment

Compliance, Finance, Operations, and two separate Development teams all had different concerns and requirements. A model acceptable to all five functions took 13+ calls over 8 weeks to achieve — but the upfront alignment meant zero rework after specification lock.

● Constraint

Backward Compatibility

Every existing client's dashboard experience had to remain exactly unchanged. The partnership model could not introduce any regression, visual change, or performance impact for the existing client base — only additive capability for partnership clients.

11 Learnings
Lessons & Insights
● What Worked Well

Exhaustive Discovery Before Specification

Running 13+ calls across every affected function before finalising the BRD meant the specification was battle-tested against all stakeholder constraints. Zero rework was needed after sign-off — every edge case had already been surfaced and resolved.

● What Worked Well

Visual Artefacts for Non-Technical Alignment

Entity-relationship diagrams and process flows enabled non-technical participants (compliance, finance) to understand product implications and provide informed feedback. Cited as enabling faster alignment than text specifications alone.

● What Worked Well

Decision Documentation With Rationale

Every product decision was documented with date, rationale, and alternatives considered. When an alternative was proposed weeks later by a stakeholder who missed the original session, the documented rationale enabled rapid resolution without re-litigation.

● Key Learning

Include Compliance From Day One

Initial design work proceeded without compliance input, leading to a weeks-long impasse when they raised concerns late. Including regulatory stakeholders from the first ideation session would have surfaced constraints immediately, saving an entire rework cycle.

● Key Learning

Validate Data Quality Before Designing Migration

The assumption that existing data was clean enough for automated migration proved wrong. Future cross-system projects should include data profiling as a discovery-phase deliverable before finalising the product approach.

● Reflection

The Invisible 90%

The user-visible deliverable was a filter dropdown. But the real value — the work that consumed 90% of the effort — was the product model, the compliance framework, and the stakeholder alignment beneath it. This project reinforced that the most impactful product work is often invisible to end users: it's the framework that makes everything built on top trustworthy and scalable.
