I led the integration of a third-party KYC/AML compliance screening platform into the payments ecosystem, automating sanctions, PEP, and adverse media checks across all payment flows to eliminate manual screening bottlenecks.

5 Overview
Executive Summary

I integrated a third-party KYC/AML compliance screening platform into the education payments ecosystem. As transaction volumes grew and the platform expanded into new jurisdictions, the existing manual screening processes became unsustainable and created regulatory risk through inconsistent coverage.

I selected a provider offering real-time API-based screening against global sanctions lists, PEP databases, and adverse media sources. I designed the integration to operate as an automated gate within the payment processing pipeline — screening payer details at transaction initiation and routing results through configurable decision logic (auto-approve, auto-reject, or manual review queue).

I structured the phased rollout approach to allow the compliance team to calibrate screening sensitivity before full deployment, reducing false positive rates to acceptable levels while maintaining comprehensive coverage. I delivered the integration without disrupting existing payment processing throughput.

I facilitated workshops with compliance officers to define screening rules: match sensitivity thresholds, escalation criteria, transaction type triggers, and manual review queue routing logic. I mapped regulatory requirements to configuration parameters.

Sandbox Integration & Validation
I connected the provider sandbox environment. I executed test scenarios covering sanctions matches, PEP hits, adverse media flags, and clear results. I measured false positive rates and calibrated sensitivity thresholds.

Screening Rules Configuration
I configured production screening rules based on sandbox learnings. I tuned fuzzy match sensitivity to balance detection rates against false positive volumes. I finalised auto-approve, auto-reject, and manual review criteria.

I deployed the integration on a limited set of payment corridors for validation. The compliance team monitored results in real-time. I tracked screening performance metrics against manual process benchmarks.

I integrated the manual review queue with operations team workflow. I connected alert routing, escalation procedures, and case resolution tracking to existing compliance tooling.
Phase 3
Full Production Rollout
I enabled screening across all payment channels: API clients, student portals, and partner integrations. I deployed real-time monitoring dashboards for compliance team visibility.
Phase 3
Performance Optimisation
I optimised screening latency to ensure payment processing throughput was maintained. I implemented caching strategies for repeat-payer scenarios. I configured batch screening for bulk payment files.

12 Learnings
Lessons & Insights
● What Worked Well

Phased Rollout for Sensitivity Calibration

Deploying on limited corridors first allowed the compliance team to observe real-world screening results and tune match sensitivity before full deployment. This prevented either excessive false positives (blocking legitimate students) or insufficient detection (compliance risk).

● What Worked Well

Compliance-Product Collaboration

Early workshops between compliance officers and the product team ensured screening rules reflected actual regulatory requirements rather than over-conservative interpretations. This balanced compliance rigour with operational efficiency from day one.

● Challenge

False Positive Management

Initial screening sensitivity generated more manual review volume than the compliance team could process efficiently. Iterative tuning was required across multiple cycles before reaching operationally sustainable false positive rates without compromising detection quality.

● Key Learning

Compliance Requirements Change Frequently

Screening rules needed updates far more frequently than initially anticipated as sanctions lists evolved and regulatory guidance shifted. Building configurability into the rules engine from the start proved essential for long-term maintainability.

● Risk Identified

Single Provider Dependency

Reliance on a single screening provider creates concentration risk. If the provider experiences downtime, payment processing is blocked. Future architecture should include provider failover capabilities or graceful degradation patterns.

● Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Would establish quantitative false positive rate targets upfront with the compliance team before deployment, rather than discovering acceptable thresholds through iteration. A clear "no more than X% manual review rate" target would have accelerated the calibration process significantly.
